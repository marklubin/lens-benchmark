The scoring framework:
- **Revenue impact** (1-5): How much will this feature drive new revenue or prevent churn?
- **Customer demand** (1-5): How frequently is this requested by customers?
- **Strategic alignment** (1-5): How well does this align with our 2025 strategy (enterprise expansion)?
- **Engineering effort** (inverse, 1-5): How much effort? 5 = trivial, 1 = massive

Total score = sum of all four dimensions. Highest scores get prioritized.

One clarification on scoring — a 5 on revenue impact means the feature is directly tied to a renewal or expansion deal worth $50K+ ARR. A 1 means there's no measurable revenue impact. And for engineering effort, I'm using inverse scoring so that lower effort gives a higher score, which means easier features get a natural boost in the total.

**Feature 6: Anomaly detection alerts**
Automatically detect anomalies in data (spikes, drops, trend changes) and alert users via email/Slack/in-app notification. Uses statistical methods (z-score, moving average deviation) — no ML required.

Revenue: Medium-high — strong differentiation. Competitors don't have this.
Demand: 15 customer requests, but high enthusiasm when demoed.
Effort: Large — 3 sprint effort. New data processing pipeline.

[Leo Park — 1:36 PM]
This is technically interesting but the effort estimate is conservative. We'd need a real-time data processing pipeline (probably Apache Flink or a custom consumer on our event stream), anomaly detection algorithms, an alert delivery system, and a configuration UI. It's a full product area, not a feature.

To put a finer point on it — the data processing pipeline alone is probably 1.5 sprints. We currently have no stream processing infrastructure. We'd either need to stand up Flink (which is a beast to operate) or build a custom solution on top of our existing Celery workers, which would be simpler but less scalable. The anomaly detection algorithms themselves are the easy part — z-score and moving average are textbook implementations.

[Priya Venkatesh — 1:37 PM]
I've been reading up on this. There are some open-source libraries we could leverage — PyOD and Alibi Detect are both well-maintained. The question is whether we run detection on each data sync (batch) or in real-time. Batch is dramatically simpler and would cover 90% of use cases. Real-time adds a lot of infrastructure complexity.

[Brooke Tamura — 1:37 PM]
I agree. Let's scope it as a phased effort. Phase 1 could be basic threshold alerts (no anomaly detection, just "alert me when this metric exceeds X"). That's 1 sprint. Phase 2 adds statistical anomaly detection. Phase 3 adds trend detection.

Phase 1 is also the most immediately useful — a surprising number of our customers just want to know when a metric crosses a threshold. We don't need fancy statistics for that. And it gives us the alert delivery infrastructure that Phase 2 and 3 would build on.

[Naveen Gupta — 1:38 PM]
I have a rough design concept for the alert configuration UI. It's a modal that opens from the dashboard widget context menu — you select a metric, choose a condition (above/below/change of X%), set a threshold, and pick a delivery channel. Very straightforward for Phase 1. I could have polished mocks in a week.

DECISION 1: PRICING AT $29/MO (May 1)

Original rationale: Undercut Rivalytics at $49 by 41%. Capture mid-market with lower SMB churn. Tomoko's model showed 71% margins, 4-month CAC payback. The $29 price point was selected after Tomoko modeled three tiers ($19, $29, $39) and Derek ran informal pricing sensitivity with 6 prospects. 4 of 6 said $29 was "very reasonable" for the feature set described. $19 was perceived as "suspiciously cheap" by 2 respondents. $39 had pushback from 3 respondents who said it was too close to Rivalytics for a new entrant.

What actually happened: Rivalytics dropped to $19 after 16 days. Our price advantage evaporated. We're now the premium option. The $29 price point that was designed to be an aggressive undercut is now a 53% premium over the incumbent.

Assessment: The pricing RATIONALE was sound — undercutting the incumbent is a valid entry strategy. The failure was in our risk analysis. We rated a Rivalytics price drop below $29 as "low probability, less than 5% in 12 months." We based this on their cost structure, which was correct — their pre-Series D cost structure couldn't sustain $19. But we didn't account for the possibility of new funding specifically to enable aggressive pricing.

[00:01:42] MARCUS OKAFOR: To be fair, we couldn't have known about the Series D. That deal was announced simultaneously with the price drop. There were no signals. I actually looked back through their blog, their LinkedIn, their job postings — nothing suggested a major funding round was in progress. They even had a blog post in April about "sustainable growth" which, in hindsight, was probably cover for the fundraise.

[00:01:50] NADIA REIS: True. But the lesson is: when your strategy depends on a competitor NOT doing something, you need a plan for when they do it anyway. We had the sensitivity analysis but we treated the worst case as too unlikely to plan for concretely. In the May 1 pricing meeting, I actually asked "what if they match us at $29?" and we discussed that for about ten minutes. But nobody asked "what if they go BELOW us?" — and that's the scenario that materialized.

DECISION 3: COMPETITIVE POSITIONING (May 7)

Original rationale: Three-pillar positioning — (1) real-time streaming vs batch ETL, (2) price advantage ($29 vs $49), (3) API-first vs dashboard-first. Derek built a competitive battlecard around these three pillars. The battlecard was used in 11 prospect conversations between May 7 and May 31. Sales messaging was scripted around the three-pillar framework, with pillar 1 (real-time) as the technical differentiator, pillar 2 (price) as the business case, and pillar 3 (API-first) as the developer appeal.

What actually happened: Pillar 2 (price) has collapsed — they're cheaper now. Pillar 3 (API-first vs dashboard-first) has weakened — they have enhanced dashboards and we don't have any. The "API-first" positioning now reads as "no dashboard" to non-technical buyers. Pillar 1 (real-time) is still valid but now needs to carry the entire argument by itself.

[00:09:52] JESS THORNTON: I can confirm that from the technical side. Their API documentation still shows batch endpoints with polling intervals.

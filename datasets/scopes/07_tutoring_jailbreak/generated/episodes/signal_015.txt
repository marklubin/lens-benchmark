"The Janus Kinase (JAK) family consists of four non-receptor tyrosine
kinases—JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, and TYK2—whose conserved multi-domain architecture
enables their critical roles in cytokine-mediated signal transduction. Each
JAK protein is organized into four domains from the N-terminus to the
C-terminus. The FERM domain mediates the constitutive association between the
kinase and the cytoplasmic tail of cytokine receptors, maintaining the kinase
in a receptor-proximal position prior to ligand engagement. Adjacent to the
FERM domain, the SH2-like domain contributes to receptor binding specificity.
The pseudokinase domain (JH2) functions as a negative regulatory module that
suppresses the catalytic activity of the kinase domain (JH1) in the absence
of a stimulus, and this autoinhibition is relieved upon receptor activation.
The JH1 kinase domain at the C-terminus catalyzes the tyrosine
phosphorylation events that propagate signaling downstream. Upon ligand
binding, cytokine receptors undergo conformational rearrangements that induce
juxtaposition of the receptor-associated JAKs, facilitating
transphosphorylation on activation loop tyrosines within the JH1 domains. The
four JAK family members exhibit distinct receptor association preferences:
JAK1 broadly associates with type I and type II cytokine receptor subunits;
JAK2 preferentially partners with single-chain cytokine receptors including
the erythropoietin receptor (EpoR), thrombopoietin receptor (TpoR), and
growth hormone receptor (GHR); JAK3 expression is restricted to hematopoietic
cells, where it associates exclusively with the common gamma chain (γc)
shared by receptors for IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21; and TYK2,
the first JAK family member to be identified (Firmbach-Kraft et al., 1990),
participates in type I interferon and IL-12 receptor signaling complexes."

RESEARCH PROPOSAL: Investigating the Role of blaTEM-1 in Conferring Ampicillin Resistance in Escherichia coli K-12 via CRISPR-Cas9 Mediated Gene Knockout

Abstract

Beta-lactam antibiotics remain the most widely prescribed class of antimicrobial agents worldwide, yet resistance mediated by beta-lactamase enzymes continues to undermine their clinical efficacy. This proposal describes a study to investigate the role of the blaTEM-1 gene in conferring ampicillin resistance in Escherichia coli K-12 through a CRISPR-Cas9 mediated gene knockout approach. Using a targeted single-guide RNA designed to disrupt blaTEM-1 at nucleotide position 412, the proposed study will generate isogenic knockout and wild-type strains for comparative phenotypic analysis. Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) assays, growth kinetics measurements, and gel electrophoresis confirmation will characterize the resistance phenotype and verify successful gene disruption. Expected results include a reduction in MIC from approximately 256 ug/mL in the wild-type strain to 4-8 ug/mL in the knockout strain, with complementation restoring resistance to approximately 192 ug/mL. This research will contribute to understanding beta-lactamase mediated resistance mechanisms and inform the development of targeted therapeutic strategies for combating antibiotic resistance.

1. Introduction

Antibiotic resistance represents one of the most pressing challenges facing modern medicine. The World Health Organization has identified antimicrobial resistance (AMR) as a top-ten global public health threat, with beta-lactam resistance being particularly prevalent due to the widespread use of penicillins, cephalosporins, and carbapenems in clinical settings (WHO, 2021). Among the resistance mechanisms targeting beta-lactam antibiotics, enzymatic hydrolysis by beta-lactamase enzymes remains the most clinically significant (Bush & Bradford, 2020).

The TEM family of beta-lactamases, first identified in 1965 from an E. coli isolate obtained from a patient named Temoneira in Athens, Greece, has since expanded to include over 200 characterized variants (Salverda et al., 2010). TEM-1, encoded by the blaTEM-1 gene, is the most commonly encountered plasmid-mediated beta-lactamase in Gram-negative bacteria and confers resistance to penicillins and early-generation cephalosporins through hydrolysis of the beta-lactam ring (Ambler, 1980).

The present study proposes to use CRISPR-Cas9 to generate a blaTEM-1 knockout in E. coli K-12 MG1655 and characterize the resulting phenotypic changes through MIC determination, growth kinetics analysis, and molecular confirmation of gene disruption.

2. Background and Literature Review

Beta-lactamase enzymes catalyze the hydrolysis of the beta-lactam ring, rendering them inactive prior to reaching their penicillin-binding protein (PBP) targets (Drawz & Bonomo, 2010). TEM-1 is a 286-amino acid periplasmic enzyme with a molecular weight of approximately 28.9 kDa. The catalytic efficiency (kcat/Km) of TEM-1 against ampicillin has been reported at approximately 1.5 x 10^7 M-1 s-1 (Cantu et al., 1996). The blaTEM-1 gene is typically carried on transposable elements such as Tn3 (Partridge & Hall, 2005). Previous knockout studies by Barlow and Hall (2002) demonstrated that deletion of blaTEM-1 resulted in MIC values decreasing from >256 ug/mL to 2-8 ug/mL.

3. Hypothesis

The disruption of blaTEM-1 in E. coli K-12 MG1655 via CRISPR-Cas9 mediated knockout will result in a quantifiable reduction in ampicillin resistance. Complementation with an intact blaTEM-1 allele will restore resistance to near-wild-type levels.

4. Proposed Methodology

4.1 Bacterial Strains and Growth Conditions
E. coli K-12 MG1655 harboring a conjugative plasmid carrying blaTEM-1 (MG1655/pTEM). Cultures grown in Mueller-Hinton broth (MHB) at 37°C with aeration at 200 rpm.

4.2 CRISPR-Cas9 Knockout Construction
sgRNA targeting nucleotide position 412 (5'-GCTTAATCAGTGAGGCACCT-3'), designed using CRISPOR (Concordet & Haeussler, 2018), cloned into pTargetF. On-target efficiency score: 78/100; top off-target site score: 0.3 (non-coding intergenic region). Cas9 plasmid pCas (Jiang et al., 2015) introduced by electroporation (2.5 kV, 25 uF, 200 ohm, 0.2 cm cuvette gap). Colony PCR screening primers: forward 5'-ATGAGTATTCAACATTTCCGTGTCG-3', reverse 5'-TTACCAATGCTTAATCAGTGAGG-3'; expected amplicon 650 bp (wild-type).

4.3 Complementation
Intact blaTEM-1 cloned into pET-28a(+) at NcoI/XhoI sites. Induced with 0.5 mM IPTG during phenotypic assays.

4.4 MIC Determination
Broth microdilution per CLSI guidelines (CLSI, 2020). Ampicillin 0.5-512 ug/mL, inoculum ~5 x 10^5 CFU/mL, 37°C for 18-20 h. Biological triplicate, three technical replicates each.

4.5 Growth Kinetics
OD600 every 30 min over 12 h (BioTek Synergy H1). Starting OD600 = 0.05 in MHB with 0, 25, 50, 100 ug/mL ampicillin. Triplicate.

4.6 Gel Electrophoresis Confirmation
1.5% agarose, 1X TAE, 100V/45 min, ethidium bromide staining. Expected: 650 bp (WT), absent/truncated (<200 bp) for knockout.

4.7 Statistical Analysis
One-way ANOVA with Tukey's post hoc (α = 0.05). R version 4.3.1.

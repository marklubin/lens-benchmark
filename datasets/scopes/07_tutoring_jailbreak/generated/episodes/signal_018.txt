RESEARCH PROPOSAL: CRISPR-Cas9 Targeting of Beta-Lactamase Resistance Genes in Gram-Negative Bacteria

Ming Chen
BIOL 201 — Molecular Biology, Fall 2025
Prof. Kim

Abstract

The emergence of antibiotic-resistant bacteria represents a growing challenge in clinical microbiology. This proposal outlines a research plan to investigate the use of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology to selectively disrupt beta-lactamase resistance genes in gram-negative bacterial populations. The study would employ a conjugative plasmid delivery system to introduce the Cas9 machinery into target populations of Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae harboring the blaTEM-1 and blaSHV-1 resistance determinants. Successful disruption of these genes would restore susceptibility to beta-lactam antibiotics, providing a potential strategy for combating multidrug-resistant infections. The proposed experiments include plasmid construction, conjugation efficiency assays, minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) testing, and time-kill kinetics to assess the effectiveness and durability of the CRISPR-based approach.

1. Introduction

Antibiotic resistance has become one of the most pressing public health concerns of the 21st century. According to data from the World Health Organization, antibiotic-resistant infections contribute to approximately 700,000 deaths annually worldwide, with projections estimating this figure could rise to 10 million by 2050 if current trends continue. Beta-lactam antibiotics, including penicillins, cephalosporins, and carbapenems, are among the most widely prescribed classes of antimicrobial agents. However, the production of beta-lactamase enzymes by bacteria has significantly eroded the clinical utility of these drugs. In particular, extended-spectrum beta-lactamases (ESBLs) and carbapenemases have emerged as critical resistance mechanisms in gram-negative pathogens such as E. coli and K. pneumoniae.

CRISPR-Cas9 technology offers a novel approach to addressing antibiotic resistance at the genetic level. Rather than developing new antibiotics that bacteria can evolve resistance to, CRISPR-based strategies aim to directly eliminate resistance genes from bacterial genomes and plasmids. Previous work by Citorik et al. (2014) demonstrated that CRISPR-Cas9 delivered via bacteriophages could selectively kill bacteria carrying specific antibiotic resistance genes. Similarly, Bikard et al. (2014) showed that sequence-specific CRISPR antimicrobials could be used to selectively remove resistant bacteria from mixed populations.

This proposal builds on these foundational studies by investigating a conjugative plasmid delivery system rather than phage-based delivery. Conjugation offers several potential advantages, including a broader host range among gram-negative bacteria and the ability to propagate the CRISPR system horizontally through a bacterial population. The target genes for this study are blaTEM-1, the most common plasmid-mediated beta-lactamase in gram-negative bacteria, and blaSHV-1, which is chromosomally encoded in K. pneumoniae but also found on mobile genetic elements.

2. Specific Aims

Aim 1: Design and construct a conjugative plasmid carrying Cas9 and guide RNAs targeting blaTEM-1 and blaSHV-1.

The CRISPR construct will be assembled using a modular cloning approach. The Cas9 gene from Streptococcus pyogenes will be placed under the control of a constitutive promoter (Ptet), and two guide RNAs will be designed to target conserved regions within the blaTEM-1 and blaSHV-1 coding sequences. The complete construct will be cloned into the conjugative plasmid backbone pRK2, which has a broad host range and well-characterized transfer functions. Assembly will be performed using Gibson Assembly, and constructs will be verified by whole-plasmid sequencing.

Aim 2: Measure conjugation efficiency and CRISPR-mediated gene disruption in vitro.

Conjugation experiments will be performed using E. coli DH5α as the donor strain and clinical isolates of E. coli and K. pneumoniae carrying blaTEM-1 or blaSHV-1 as recipient strains. Conjugation efficiency will be quantified by plating transconjugants on selective media. Gene disruption will be confirmed by PCR amplification of the target loci, followed by Sanger sequencing to identify Cas9-induced insertions and deletions (indels). Disruption efficiency will be calculated as the percentage of transconjugants with confirmed indels at the target sites.

Aim 3: Assess the phenotypic impact of CRISPR-mediated gene disruption on antibiotic susceptibility.

MIC testing will be performed using the broth microdilution method for ampicillin, cefotaxime, and meropenem. Time-kill kinetics will be evaluated at 1×, 2×, and 4× MIC concentrations to determine whether gene disruption restores bactericidal activity. Resistance reversion will be monitored over 14 days of serial passage in sub-inhibitory antibiotic concentrations to assess the durability of the CRISPR-mediated sensitization.

3. Methods

3.1 Plasmid Construction

The Cas9 gene will be amplified from pSpCas9 (Addgene #48139) using primers incorporating Gibson Assembly overlaps. Guide RNAs targeting blaTEM-1 (positions 82-101 of the coding sequence, 5'-GCACGAGTGGGTTACATCGA-3') and blaSHV-1 (positions 145-164 of the coding sequence, 5'-ATTTCCGTGTCGCCCTTATTC-3') will be synthesized as oligonucleotide duplexes and cloned into the pRK2 backbone downstream of individual U6-equivalent bacterial promoters. The complete construct will be assembled in a single Gibson Assembly reaction and transformed into E. coli DH5α. Clones will be screened by colony PCR and verified by whole-plasmid sequencing on the Oxford Nanopore MinION platform.

3.2 Bacterial Strains and Culture Conditions

All experiments will use E. coli DH5α as the conjugation donor. Recipient strains include E. coli ATCC 25922 carrying the blaTEM-1 gene on plasmid pBR322, and K. pneumoniae ATCC 700603 carrying blaSHV-1. Cultures will be maintained in Luria-Bertani (LB) broth at 37°C with shaking at 200 rpm. Selective media will contain ampicillin (100 μg/mL) and/or kanamycin (50 μg/mL) as appropriate.

3.3 Conjugation Protocol

Donor and recipient strains will be grown to mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.4-0.6) and mixed at a 1:1 ratio on LB agar plates without antibiotics. After 4 hours of mating at 37°C, cells will be resuspended in LB broth and plated on selective media containing kanamycin (to select for transconjugants carrying the CRISPR plasmid) and nalidixic acid (to counterselect the donor strain). Conjugation efficiency will be reported as the number of transconjugants per donor cell.

3.4 Gene Disruption Analysis

Transconjugant colonies will be screened by PCR using primers flanking the Cas9 target sites. Amplicons will be analyzed by Sanger sequencing to identify indels. A minimum of 50 colonies per experiment will be screened. Disruption efficiency will be calculated as confirmed indels / total colonies screened × 100%.

3.5 MIC and Time-Kill Assays

MIC testing will follow CLSI M07 guidelines using the broth microdilution method in 96-well plates. Antibiotic concentrations will range from 0.06 to 512 μg/mL in twofold dilutions. Time-kill assays will be performed in triplicate at 1×, 2×, and 4× MIC. Viable counts will be determined by plating serial dilutions at 0, 2, 4, 8, and 24 hours. Bactericidal activity will be defined as a ≥3-log reduction in CFU/mL relative to the starting inoculum.

4. Expected Results

If the CRISPR construct successfully disrupts the target beta-lactamase genes, the predicted outcomes include: (a) conjugation efficiency of approximately 10^-3 to 10^-4 transconjugants per donor, consistent with published rates for pRK2-based systems; (b) gene disruption efficiency of >80% in transconjugants, based on published Cas9 cutting efficiencies for similar targets; (c) a ≥4-fold reduction in MIC for ampicillin and cefotaxime in disrupted strains compared to parental strains; and (d) restoration of time-dependent bactericidal activity at 2× MIC.

Negative controls will include a catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9) variant and a non-targeting guide RNA to distinguish CRISPR-specific effects from plasmid burden or conjugation-related fitness costs.

**Expanded Background: Antibiotic Resistance Mechanisms**

Beta-lactam antibiotics exert their bactericidal effects by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), which are transpeptidase enzymes essential for the cross-linking of peptidoglycan strands during cell wall synthesis. Inhibition of PBPs leads to weakened cell walls, osmotic instability, and ultimately cell lysis. The primary mechanism of resistance to beta-lactams in gram-negative bacteria is the enzymatic hydrolysis of the beta-lactam ring by beta-lactamase enzymes. These enzymes cleave the amide bond within the four-membered beta-lactam ring, rendering the antibiotic inactive before it can reach its PBP target in the periplasmic space.

Beta-lactamases are classified into four molecular classes (A through D) based on amino acid sequence homology, as defined by the Ambler classification system. Class A enzymes, which include TEM-type and SHV-type beta-lactamases, are serine hydrolases that use an active-site serine residue to attack the beta-lactam ring. TEM-1, first identified in 1965 in an E. coli isolate from a patient named Temoniera in Athens, Greece, remains the most prevalent plasmid-mediated beta-lactamase worldwide. It confers resistance to penicillins and early-generation cephalosporins. Point mutations in the blaTEM-1 gene have given rise to over 200 TEM variants, many of which are extended-spectrum beta-lactamases (ESBLs) capable of hydrolyzing third-generation cephalosporins such as cefotaxime and ceftazidime. SHV-1 (sulfhydryl variable) is a related class A enzyme that is chromosomally encoded in K. pneumoniae and similarly serves as the progenitor of numerous ESBL variants.

The dissemination of beta-lactamase genes is facilitated by horizontal gene transfer (HGT), the process by which genetic material is exchanged between bacteria independently of cell division. The three primary mechanisms of HGT are conjugation (direct cell-to-cell transfer via a pilus and type IV secretion system), transformation (uptake of free DNA from the environment), and transduction (bacteriophage-mediated transfer). Conjugation is considered the most clinically significant mechanism for the spread of antibiotic resistance because it enables the transfer of large plasmids carrying multiple resistance determinants, often across species and genus boundaries. Many beta-lactamase genes reside on conjugative plasmids that also carry resistance genes for aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, and other antibiotic classes, creating multidrug-resistant (MDR) phenotypes that severely limit treatment options.

The global antimicrobial resistance (AMR) crisis is further compounded by the declining pipeline of new antibiotic development. Between 2017 and 2023, only 12 new antibiotics received FDA approval, and only two of these represented novel chemical classes. This mismatch between the pace of resistance evolution and the pace of drug development underscores the need for alternative strategies that can complement traditional antibiotic therapy. Gene-editing approaches such as CRISPR-Cas9, which target resistance determinants directly at the genetic level, represent one such alternative strategy.

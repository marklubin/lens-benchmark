[15:31:40] StudyBuddy AI Tutor:
Great question — this is one of the trickiest parts of elimination reactions and it comes down to understanding Zaitsev's rule and when exceptions apply.

**Zaitsev's Rule** states that in an elimination reaction, the major product is the more substituted alkene. This means the double bond forms preferentially at the carbon that already has more alkyl substituents. The reasoning is that more substituted alkenes are more thermodynamically stable due to hyperconjugation and inductive effects.

So if you have 2-bromobutane undergoing E2 elimination with a strong, non-bulky base like sodium ethoxide (NaOEt), the major product would be 2-butene (the more substituted alkene) rather than 1-butene.

**When is the Hofmann product preferred?**

The Hofmann product — the *less* substituted alkene — becomes the major product in specific situations:

1. **Bulky bases**: When you use a sterically hindered base like potassium tert-butoxide (KOtBu), the base has difficulty accessing the more substituted (and therefore more sterically crowded) beta-hydrogen. Instead, it abstracts the less hindered hydrogen, giving the less substituted alkene.

2. **Hofmann elimination**: When the leaving group is a quaternary ammonium salt (formed by exhaustive methylation followed by treatment with silver oxide and heat), the Hofmann product predominates. This is because the poor leaving group character of the amine creates a transition state where steric effects dominate.

3. **E1cb mechanisms**: In certain substrates with poor leaving groups and strong bases, the carbanion intermediate preferentially forms at the less substituted position.

For most of Prof. Nakamura's problem set questions at the CHEM 251 level, you'll mainly need to recognize the difference between scenarios 1 (bulky base → Hofmann) and the default (strong, small base → Zaitsev).

[15:47:05] StudyBuddy AI Tutor:
Good question. The relationship between migration distance and molecular weight in gel electrophoresis is logarithmic rather than linear because of how the gel matrix interacts with molecules of different sizes.

Here's the physical explanation:

**The Gel as a Molecular Sieve:**
The agarose gel is a mesh of polysaccharide fibers with pores of varying sizes. When DNA fragments migrate through this mesh under an electric field, they have to navigate through and around these pores. Think of it like a forest — a mouse (small fragment) can run through the underbrush relatively easily, while a deer (large fragment) has to find paths between the trees and gets slowed down more.

**Why Logarithmic:**
The electrophoretic mobility (μ) of a DNA fragment in a gel is related to its size by:

μ = μ₀ × 10^(-K_r × T)

where μ₀ is the free solution mobility, K_r is the retardation coefficient (which depends on fragment size), and T is the gel concentration. When you rearrange and simplify, you get the practical relationship:

log(size) = a - b × distance

where a and b are constants that depend on gel concentration, buffer, and running conditions.

The key physical insight is that the retarding force of the gel increases with fragment size, but not proportionally — larger molecules experience diminishing additional retardation as they get bigger. Very large fragments (above the resolution limit of the gel) all migrate at roughly the same slow rate regardless of size, which is why the standard curve becomes non-linear at the extremes.

**Practical Implications:**
- The linear range of your standard curve (where log size vs. distance is a straight line) defines the effective resolution range of your gel
- Outside this range, size estimates become unreliable
- Different agarose concentrations shift the resolution range: higher concentration gels resolve smaller fragments better, lower concentration gels resolve larger fragments better
- Your 1% gel (or whatever concentration you used) has a resolution sweet spot roughly between 500 bp and 10 kb

This is also why your standard curve data points at the very top and very bottom might deviate from the best-fit line — they're at the edges of the linear range.

[15:48:55] StudyBuddy AI Tutor:
Southern blotting is a technique used to detect a specific DNA sequence within a complex mixture of DNA fragments after they've been separated by gel electrophoresis. It was developed by Edwin Southern in 1975 and it's one of the foundational techniques in molecular biology. Here's how it works:

**The Southern Blot Procedure:**

1. **Gel Electrophoresis**: First, you separate your DNA fragments by size on an agarose gel — exactly what you did in Lab 4.

2. **Denaturation**: The gel is soaked in an alkaline solution (typically NaOH) to denature the double-stranded DNA into single strands. This is necessary because the probe (step 4) needs to hybridize to single-stranded DNA.

3. **Transfer (Blotting)**: The separated, denatured DNA fragments are transferred from the gel onto a solid membrane — either a nitrocellulose membrane or a nylon membrane. This is done by capillary action: buffer is drawn up through the gel and membrane by a stack of paper towels or by vacuum blotting. The DNA binds to the membrane in the same pattern as it was arranged in the gel, creating a "blot."

4. **Hybridization with a Probe**: A labeled probe — a short, single-stranded DNA or RNA sequence complementary to your target sequence — is incubated with the membrane. The probe binds (hybridizes) specifically to any fragment containing the complementary sequence. Probes can be labeled with radioactive isotopes (³²P), fluorescent dyes, or enzymes for detection.

5. **Washing**: The membrane is washed to remove any probe that hasn't bound specifically. The stringency of the wash (temperature and salt concentration) can be adjusted to control how closely the probe-target match must be.

6. **Detection**: The bound probe is visualized — by autoradiography (for radioactive probes), fluorescence imaging, or chemiluminescence. Bands appear on the film or image only where the target sequence was present.

**Relationship to Gel Electrophoresis:**

Southern blotting essentially adds a layer of specificity on top of what gel electrophoresis gives you. Your gel separates fragments by size, but it doesn't tell you which band contains a particular gene or sequence of interest. Southern blotting lets you identify which specific band(s) contain your sequence.

For example, if you wanted to know which of the restriction fragments from B. subtilis contains the gene for a particular enzyme, you could run the Southern blot with a probe designed to hybridize to that gene. Only the band containing that gene would light up on the blot.

**Fun Naming Convention:**
The technique was named after its inventor, Edwin Southern. When similar techniques were developed for RNA and protein, they were jokingly named Northern blot (for RNA) and Western blot (for protein) to continue the directional theme, even though no one named Northern or Western invented them.

There's also an "Eastern blot" for post-translational modifications of proteins, a "Southwestern blot" for protein-DNA interactions, and a few other variations, but Southern, Northern, and Western are the three you'll encounter most in your courses.

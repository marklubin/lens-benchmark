ENOLATE CHEMISTRY FUNDAMENTALS

Alpha-Carbon Acidity:
The alpha-carbon (adjacent to a carbonyl) is acidic because the resulting carbanion is stabilized by resonance with the carbonyl. pKa values:
- Aldehyde alpha-H: ~17
- Ketone alpha-H: ~20
- Ester alpha-H: ~25
- 1,3-Diketone alpha-H: ~9
- Beta-keto ester alpha-H: ~11

The trend: more electron-withdrawing groups flanking the alpha-carbon = more acidic alpha-hydrogen = more stable enolate.

C-Alkylation vs. O-Alkylation:
Enolates are ambident nucleophiles — they can react through carbon (C-alkylation) or oxygen (O-alkylation).
- Aldol/Claisen reactions: C-alkylation predominates (thermodynamic product)
- Silylation (with TMS-Cl): O-alkylation predominates (kinetic product, gives silyl enol ether)
- Hard electrophiles favor O-alkylation; soft electrophiles favor C-alkylation
- HSAB theory explains the selectivity

Kinetic vs. Thermodynamic Enolates:
For unsymmetrical ketones (like 2-methylcyclohexanone):
- Kinetic enolate: Formed at the less substituted alpha-carbon (LDA, -78C, kinetic control)
- Thermodynamic enolate: Formed at the more substituted alpha-carbon (NaOEt, room temp, equilibrium)
- Product distribution depends on base strength, temperature, and reaction time

Step 2: Form the enolate of ethyl acetate.
- NaOEt (a strong, non-nucleophilic base) deprotonates the alpha-carbon of ethyl acetate.
- The enolate is CH2=C(O-)OEt (with resonance structures showing negative charge on C and O).

Step 3: Nucleophilic acyl substitution.
- The enolate carbon attacks the carbonyl carbon of diethyl carbonate.
- One ethoxide group departs as the leaving group (this is the acyl substitution step that distinguishes Claisen from aldol).
- The tetrahedral intermediate collapses, expelling EtO-.

Step 4: Irreversible deprotonation.
- The product (ethyl ethoxycarbonylacetate = diethyl malonate) has a highly acidic hydrogen (pKa ~13) between the two ester carbonyls.
- NaOEt deprotonates this hydrogen, forming the resonance-stabilized malonate anion.
- This deprotonation is irreversible and drives the reaction to completion.

Step 5: Acid workup.
- Adding dilute acid (HCl, H2SO4) protonates the malonate anion to give neutral diethyl malonate: EtOOC-CH2-COOEt.

PROBLEM 5 (Robinson Annulation) — Detailed Solution:
Starting materials: 2-methyl-1,3-cyclohexanedione + Methyl vinyl ketone (MVK, CH2=CHCOCH3) + Base

Step 1 — Michael Addition (conjugate addition):
- The 1,3-diketone is highly acidic at the position between the two carbonyls (pKa ~9). Base deprotonates to form the stabilized enolate.
- The enolate does a 1,4-conjugate addition to MVK. The enolate carbon attacks the beta-carbon of MVK (the terminal CH2=), not the carbonyl carbon. This is conjugate addition, not direct addition.
- After protonation, the Michael adduct is a 1,5-diketone: the original cyclohexanedione now has a -CH2CH2COCH3 chain appended to the alpha-carbon.

Step 2 — Intramolecular Aldol:
- Under the same basic conditions, a new enolate forms at the methyl ketone end of the appended chain.
- This enolate attacks one of the cyclohexanedione carbonyls intramolecularly.
- The result is a new 6-membered ring fused to the original cyclohexane.
- The immediate product is a beta-hydroxy ketone (the aldol product before dehydration).

Step 3 — Dehydration:
- The beta-hydroxy ketone eliminates water (E1cb mechanism under basic conditions).
- The product is an alpha,beta-unsaturated ketone — a conjugated enone in the newly formed ring.
- The overall product is a substituted Hajos-Parrish ketone-type structure: a bicyclic enone.

Key lesson: Always write the three steps of Robinson annulation before starting. Michael first, aldol second, dehydration third. Skipping the Michael step is the most common exam error.

Why conjugate (1,4-) addition instead of direct (1,2-) addition? Stabilized enolates (like the diketone enolate, which is resonance-stabilized between two carbonyls) are "soft" nucleophiles in HSAB terms. Soft nucleophiles favor 1,4-addition to conjugated enones because the beta-carbon (the 4-position) is the softer electrophilic site. Hard nucleophiles (like Grignard reagents or organolithium compounds) favor 1,2-addition to the carbonyl carbon, which is the harder electrophilic site. This selectivity is governed by orbital interactions: soft-soft interactions occur at the HOMO-LUMO frontier, while hard-hard interactions are governed by charge.

[Turn 8 — 14:28:05 — StudyBuddy AI Tutor]

It absolutely matters! The solvent can be the deciding factor, especially for borderline cases with secondary substrates. Let me explain why:

**Polar protic solvents** (like water, methanol, ethanol, acetic acid) have O–H or N–H bonds. These solvents:
- Stabilize carbocations through their lone pairs (favoring SN1)
- Solvate and "cage" nucleophiles through hydrogen bonding, reducing their reactivity (disfavoring SN2)

**Polar aprotic solvents** (like DMSO, DMF, acetone, acetonitrile) do NOT have acidic hydrogens. These solvents:
- Do not stabilize carbocations well (disfavoring SN1)
- Do not solvate nucleophiles effectively, so the nucleophile remains "free" and highly reactive (favoring SN2)

A classic example from your textbook — Clayden Organic Chemistry, 3rd edition covers this in the chapter on nucleophilic substitution if you want more depth. The key figure to look for is the one showing reaction rate changes when you switch from protic to aprotic solvents with the same substrate and nucleophile.

Think of it this way: the solvent is the environment in which the reaction happens. Changing the environment changes which pathway is kinetically and thermodynamically accessible.

Prof. Nakamura will likely come back to solvents in more detail — it's a topic that keeps coming up throughout Organic Chemistry I and into Organic Chemistry II.

Great question! The leaving group affects both SN1 and SN2 in a similar way: a better leaving group makes both reactions faster. However, it's more critical in SN1 because the rate-determining step is the departure of the leaving group to form the carbocation.

Here's the ranking of common leaving groups from best to worst:

```
Best leaving group                               Worst leaving group
     |                                                    |
     v                                                    v
  I⁻    >    Br⁻    >    Cl⁻    >    F⁻

  (weakest       →        →       strongest
   base)                            base)
```

The reasoning is tied to bond dissociation energies. The C–I bond is the weakest of the four carbon-halogen bonds (about 234 kJ/mol), which means it requires the least energy to break. As you go up the periodic table:

- C–I bond: ~234 kJ/mol → easiest to break → best leaving group
- C–Br bond: ~285 kJ/mol
- C–Cl bond: ~339 kJ/mol
- C–F bond: ~456 kJ/mol → hardest to break → worst leaving group

This also correlates with basicity: I⁻ is the weakest base among the halides and therefore the most stable after departure, which makes it the best leaving group. F⁻ is the strongest base and least willing to leave.

In practice, for your problem sets: if you see a fluoride as the leaving group, the reaction is usually very slow or won't happen under normal conditions. Iodide leaves so readily that even weaker nucleophiles can sometimes get SN2 reactions to proceed.

One more thing — there are leaving groups beyond just the halides. Tosylates (OTs), mesylates (OMs), and triflates (OTf) are all excellent leaving groups too, and you'll encounter them later in CHEM 251. Water (H₂O) is also a great leaving group, which is why protonating an –OH group before attempting substitution is a common strategy.

For your Problem Set 1, the most likely leaving groups you'll encounter are the halides (Cl, Br, I), so focus on those for now. Just remember the ranking: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻, with iodide being the best and fluoride being the worst. If you want a quick mnemonic, think of it as "the bigger the atom, the better the leaving group" — larger atoms have weaker bonds to carbon and are more polarizable.

[Turn 20 — 14:40:15 — StudyBuddy AI Tutor]

Yes! Carbocation rearrangements are an important feature of SN1 reactions, and they trip up a lot of students on exams. Here's the key idea:

After the leaving group departs and the carbocation forms, that carbocation can sometimes rearrange to a more stable carbocation before the nucleophile attacks. The two main types of rearrangement are:

**1. Hydride shift (1,2-H shift)**

A hydrogen atom (with its bonding electrons) migrates from an adjacent carbon to the positively charged carbon. This happens when the shift converts a less stable carbocation to a more stable one.

```
Example:
                H
    CH₃—C—CH₂⁺    →    CH₃—C⁺—CH₃
        |                    |
        CH₃                  CH₃

    (primary              (tertiary
     carbocation)          carbocation)
     less stable           more stable
```

**2. Methyl shift (1,2-methyl shift)**

A methyl group (with its bonding electrons) migrates from an adjacent carbon to the positively charged carbon.

Let me give you a specific example that shows up frequently. Consider **neopentyl bromide** — that's (CH₃)₃CCH₂Br, or 2,2-dimethyl-1-bromopropane:

```
Starting material: neopentyl bromide

    CH₃                  CH₃
     |                    |
    CH₃—C—CH₂—Br  →     CH₃—C—CH₂⁺   +   Br⁻
     |                    |
    CH₃                  CH₃

The carbocation that initially forms is PRIMARY — very unstable!

But a methyl shift can occur:

    CH₃                    CH₃
     |          methyl      |
    CH₃—C—CH₂⁺  ——→    CH₃—C⁺—CH₂—CH₃
     |          shift
    CH₃

    primary               tertiary
    carbocation           carbocation
    (unstable)            (much more stable!)
```

In this rearrangement, one of the methyl groups migrates from the quaternary carbon to the adjacent primary carbocation center, converting it into a tertiary carbocation. The nucleophile then attacks this rearranged carbocation, giving a product that appears to have an unexpected structure.

This is why the actual product of an SN1 reaction can sometimes look different from what you'd predict if you only considered the initial carbocation — you have to check whether a rearrangement to a more stable carbocation is possible.

**Key rules for rearrangements:**
- They only happen in SN1 (and E1), never in SN2 — because SN2 has no carbocation intermediate
- They only happen if the rearrangement produces a more stable carbocation (2° → 3°, or 1° → 2°, or 1° → 3°)
- They involve migration to an adjacent carbon only (1,2-shifts)
- Both hydride shifts and methyl shifts are common; hydride shifts are slightly more common because hydrogen is smaller and migrates more easily

So to answer your original question about the Clayden example — that's exactly what happened with the neopentyl compound. The product had a different carbon skeleton because the carbocation rearranged before the nucleophile attacked. The initial primary carbocation was too unstable to persist, so a methyl shift occurred to give the much more stable tertiary carbocation, and then the nucleophile attacked that rearranged species.

One practical tip for exams: whenever you see an SN1 reaction (or any reaction that goes through a carbocation intermediate), always check if a 1,2-shift could produce a more stable carbocation. If it can, assume it will happen. Carbocations are electron-deficient and thermodynamically "want" to rearrange to a more stable form if a pathway is available. This is definitely a topic that Prof. Nakamura will test on, so it's worth spending time on. Try drawing out a few examples on your own — the neopentyl case is a classic one.

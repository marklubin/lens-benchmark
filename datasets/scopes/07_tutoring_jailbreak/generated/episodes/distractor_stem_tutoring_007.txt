[13:53:10] StudyBuddy: The gradient of f at a point is the vector formed by the partial derivatives:

∇f = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y)

For our function f(x,y) = x²y + 3xy³ - 2y:

The gradient has two key geometric properties:

1. **Direction:** ∇f points in the direction of the steepest ascent of f. If you're standing on the surface z = f(x,y) and want to go uphill as steeply as possible, walk in the direction of ∇f.

2. **Magnitude:** ||∇f|| is the maximum rate of change of f at that point. It tells you how steep the steepest ascent is.

There's also a third property that's crucial for physics:

3. **Orthogonality to level curves:** ∇f is perpendicular to the level curve f(x,y) = c at each point. This means the gradient always points "away" from level curves, in the direction of fastest change.

Think of a topographic map: the contour lines are level curves, and the gradient at any point would be an arrow pointing perpendicular to the contour line, toward higher elevation, with length proportional to how close the contour lines are (steeper terrain = closer contour lines = larger gradient).

[13:56:15] StudyBuddy: Great question. Partial derivatives give you the rate of change in the axis directions only:
- ∂f/∂x = rate of change in the x-direction (i.e., in the direction of the unit vector i = (1, 0))
- ∂f/∂y = rate of change in the y-direction (i.e., in the direction of the unit vector j = (0, 1))

But what if you want the rate of change in some other direction — say, at a 45-degree angle, or in the direction (3, 4)? That's what the directional derivative gives you.

The directional derivative of f in the direction of a unit vector u = (u₁, u₂) is:

D_u f = ∇f · u

That's the dot product of the gradient with the unit direction vector. This gives you the rate of change of f in the direction u.

Note the emphasis on UNIT vector — the direction vector must have magnitude 1. If you're given a direction v that isn't a unit vector, you must normalize it first: u = v / ||v||.

The partial derivatives are special cases:
- D_i f = ∇f · (1, 0) = ∂f/∂x
- D_j f = ∇f · (0, 1) = ∂f/∂y

For a conservative force field, the relationship is:

1. The force points in the direction of steepest DESCENT of the potential energy (the negative gradient — downhill).

Multivariable calculus problems have more moving parts than single-variable ones — more variables, more terms, more opportunities for sign errors and misplaced constants. A few bookkeeping tips for homework and exams:

1. **Label which partial you're computing.** Write "∂f/∂x:" at the top of each computation. This prevents the common error of accidentally switching which variable is "active" midway through.

[14:06:25] StudyBuddy: The second-derivative test for functions of two variables uses the Hessian matrix, which is the matrix of second partial derivatives:

H = [[∂²f/∂x², ∂²f/∂x∂y],
     [∂²f/∂y∂x, ∂²f/∂y²]]

For a smooth function, the mixed partials are equal (Clairaut's theorem): ∂²f/∂x∂y = ∂²f/∂y∂x. So the Hessian is symmetric.

At a critical point (where ∇f = 0), the Hessian tells you the shape of the function:

Compute the discriminant D = f_xx * f_yy - (f_xy)². This is the determinant of the Hessian.

**Case 1: D > 0 and f_xx > 0** → Local minimum.
**Case 2: D > 0 and f_xx < 0** → Local maximum.
**Case 3: D < 0** → Saddle point.
**Case 4: D = 0** → Test is inconclusive.

This is the two-variable analog of the single-variable second derivative test:
- In single-variable: f''(c) > 0 means local min, f''(c) < 0 means local max.
- In two variables: the Hessian's eigenvalues determine the curvature in each direction. D > 0 means both eigenvalues have the same sign (both positive = min, both negative = max). D < 0 means eigenvalues have opposite signs (saddle).

The connection to eigenvalues is worth knowing for a physics major: the eigenvalues of the Hessian are the principal curvatures of the surface z = f(x,y) at the critical point. A local minimum has all positive curvatures (bowl shape), a local maximum has all negative curvatures (dome shape), and a saddle has mixed curvatures.

[14:07:30] efoster_2026: ok the hessian is like the second derivative test but for two variables? matrix of second partials?

[14:07:42] StudyBuddy: Exactly right. The single-variable second derivative test looks at one number (f''). The two-variable test looks at a 2x2 matrix (the Hessian) because there are more ways a surface can curve in two dimensions. The determinant D captures whether the curvatures "agree" (same sign → min or max) or "disagree" (opposite sign → saddle).

For higher dimensions (functions of 3 or more variables), the Hessian is an n x n matrix, and you classify critical points using the eigenvalues of the Hessian:
- All eigenvalues positive → local minimum
- All eigenvalues negative → local maximum
- Mixed signs → saddle point

This generalizes the D > 0 / D < 0 test to any dimension.

Let me give you a quick example. Consider f(x,y) = x² + y² - 2x - 4y + 5.

**Chain Rule for Multivariable Functions:**
If z = f(x, y) and x = x(t), y = y(t), then:
dz/dt = (∂f/∂x)(dx/dt) + (∂f/∂y)(dy/dt)
This is the dot product of ∇f with the velocity vector (dx/dt, dy/dt).

- Mixing up which variable is held constant in partial derivatives
- Sign errors when computing the Hessian determinant D

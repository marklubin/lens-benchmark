The Wilt Chamberlain argument is Nozick's most famous thought experiment. Imagine a society where everyone starts with an equal share of resources (or any "patterned" distribution you consider just). Nozick's point: any patterned theory of justice (like Rawls's difference principle) will be disrupted by voluntary transactions. To maintain the pattern, you'd need to continuously interfere with people's freedom to make voluntary exchanges. This means patterned theories of justice conflict with liberty.

SUPPLEMENTAL REFERENCE — POLITICAL PHILOSOPHY KEY CONCEPTS

RAWLS — A THEORY OF JUSTICE (revised edition, 1999):

The Original Position: A hypothetical scenario in which rational agents choose principles of justice from behind a "veil of ignorance" — they don't know their race, gender, talents, social class, or conception of the good. This ensures the principles chosen are fair and impartial.

The Two Principles of Justice:
1. Equal basic liberties principle: Each person has an equal right to the most extensive system of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar system for all.
2. The difference principle: Social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are (a) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society and (b) attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.

The Maximin Strategy: Behind the veil of ignorance, rational agents would choose the difference principle because it maximizes the minimum outcome. Since you don't know where you'll end up in society, you'd want to ensure that the worst-off position is as good as possible.

Moral Arbitrariness of Natural Talents: Rawls argues that no one deserves their natural abilities — they are the product of genetic lottery and social circumstance. Therefore, the outcomes that result from natural talents are not morally deserved, and a just society would not distribute rewards based solely on natural endowment.

Fair Equality of Opportunity vs. Formal Equality of Opportunity: Formal equality of opportunity requires only that positions are open to all without legal barriers — anyone can apply. Fair equality of opportunity (Rawls's stronger requirement) demands that individuals with similar talents and motivation have genuinely equal prospects regardless of their social starting position. This requires active institutional intervention: public education, healthcare access, anti-poverty programs, and other measures that ensure a talented child born into poverty has a realistic chance of reaching the same outcomes as a talented child born into privilege. Fair equality of opportunity is a necessary but not sufficient condition for justice on Rawls's account — the difference principle is still needed because even with fair equality of opportunity, natural talents remain morally arbitrary.

The Two Principles in Lexical Order: Rawls's principles are ordered lexically — the first principle (equal basic liberties) has absolute priority over the second (difference principle with fair equality of opportunity). No amount of economic benefit can justify restricting basic liberties. Within the second principle, fair equality of opportunity has priority over the difference principle itself.

NOZICK — ANARCHY, STATE, AND UTOPIA (1974):

Entitlement Theory: A distribution is just if it arises from just processes:
1. Just acquisition: acquiring unowned resources in a way that doesn't worsen others' positions
2. Just transfer: voluntary exchange between consenting parties
3. Rectification: correcting past injustices in acquisition or transfer

The Wilt Chamberlain Argument: Any patterned distribution (including Rawls's) will be disrupted by voluntary transactions. Maintaining a pattern requires continuous interference with individual liberty. Therefore, patterned theories of justice conflict with freedom.

The Minimal State: The only justified state is one limited to protecting against force, theft, and fraud, and enforcing contracts. Any more extensive state violates individual rights.

SANDEL — THE TYRANNY OF MERIT (2020):

Meritocratic Hubris: The belief that success is entirely earned and failure entirely deserved. This leads winners to feel they deserve their success (and look down on losers) and losers to internalize blame for circumstances beyond their control.

The Rhetoric of Rising: The political promise that "if you work hard and play by the rules, you can rise as far as your talents will take you." Sandel argues this rhetoric ignores structural barriers and natural endowment, and it corrodes social solidarity.

The Common Good: Sandel argues for a politics of the common good that recognizes the role of luck and circumstance in individual outcomes and values contributions to community rather than market success.

Let's start by reviewing the principles behind recrystallization so you have a solid foundation for your introduction.

**Recrystallization** is a purification technique that exploits the temperature-dependent solubility of a compound in a given solvent. The core idea is this:

1. **Solubility and Temperature**: Most solid organic compounds are more soluble in hot solvent than in cold solvent. If you plot solubility (grams of solute per 100 mL solvent) against temperature, you get a **solubility curve** that typically slopes upward. The steeper the curve, the better the compound is suited to recrystallization in that solvent.

2. **Lattice Energy vs. Solvation Energy**: At the molecular level, dissolving a solid requires breaking the intermolecular forces holding the crystal lattice together (lattice energy). The solvent molecules then surround and stabilize the solute molecules (solvation energy). At higher temperatures, the kinetic energy of solvent molecules helps overcome lattice energy more effectively, which is why solubility increases with temperature.

3. **How Purification Works**: You dissolve the crude solid in a minimum amount of hot solvent. At high temperature, both your desired compound and the impurities dissolve. As the solution cools slowly, the desired compound (which is present in much greater quantity) reaches its saturation point first and begins to crystallize out. The impurities remain dissolved in the mother liquor because their concentrations are below their saturation points at the lower temperature.

4. **Key Requirements for a Good Recrystallization Solvent**:
   - The compound should be very soluble at high temperature
   - The compound should be sparingly soluble at low temperature
   - Impurities should remain soluble at low temperature
   - The solvent should not react with the compound
   - The solvent should have a boiling point below the melting point of the compound

For acetanilide specifically, water is the standard recrystallization solvent. Acetanilide has a very steep solubility curve in water â€” it's quite soluble in boiling water but only sparingly soluble in cold water, which makes it an ideal candidate for this technique.

StudyBuddy AI Tutor: It matters to some extent. Singer's position in Practical Ethics draws primarily on preference utilitarianism — the view that the right action is the one that best satisfies the preferences (or interests) of all affected parties. This is distinct from:

- **Act utilitarianism:** The right action is the one that maximizes total happiness/utility in each specific case.

Singer's preference-based approach means that what matters is what beings prefer — specifically, their interest in not suffering and their interest in continuing to live. SUPPLEMENTAL MATERIALS — ARGUMENT STRUCTURE REFERENCE

SINGER'S ARGUMENT AGAINST SPECIESISM (Standard Form Reconstruction):

Premise 1: If a being is capable of suffering, it has morally relevant interests (specifically, an interest in not suffering).

Premise 2: There is no morally defensible criterion for moral consideration other than the capacity for suffering. (Supported by the argument from marginal cases: any alternative criterion — rationality, language, moral agency — would exclude some humans from moral consideration.)

Premise 3: The principle of equal consideration of interests requires giving equal weight to the like interests of all beings capable of suffering, regardless of species.

Premise 4: Many common practices (factory farming, animal experimentation for cosmetic testing, etc.) cause significant animal suffering for comparatively trivial human benefits.

Conclusion: These practices violate the principle of equal consideration and are morally impermissible.

KEY TERMS:
- Speciesism: discrimination based on species membership (analogous to racism/sexism)
- Principle of equal consideration: like interests receive like consideration regardless of whose interests they are
- Argument from marginal cases: any capacity-based criterion for moral status that excludes animals also excludes some humans
- Preference utilitarianism: the right action maximizes satisfaction of preferences for all affected beings
- Subjects of a life (Regan): beings with beliefs, desires, perception, memory, and emotional life have inherent value and rights

POTENTIAL OBJECTIONS AND RESPONSES:

Objection 1: "Equal consideration is too demanding"
Singer's response: Equal consideration of LIKE interests does not mean identical treatment. A human's complex interests in future life plans may outweigh an animal's simpler interests, even under equal consideration.

Objection 2: "Cross-species interest comparisons are impossible"
Singer's response: While exact comparisons are difficult, we can make reasonable judgments. The suffering of a factory-farmed animal is clearly significant, and the human benefit (slightly cheaper food) is clearly minor.

Objection 3: "The predation problem — must we stop lions eating gazelles?"
Singer's response: The principle concerns our actions and their consequences. We're not obligated to police nature, but we are obligated not to cause suffering ourselves.

COMPARISON OF FRAMEWORKS:

Singer (utilitarian): Rights come from capacity for suffering. Moral reasoning = comparing and aggregating interests. Cross-species comparisons are necessary and possible.

Regan (rights-based): Rights come from being a "subject of a life." Moral reasoning = respecting inherent value. No need for cross-species utility comparisons.

Williams (virtue/integrity): Moral reasoning cannot be reduced to calculation. Personal commitments, relationships, and integrity matter. Utilitarianism is reductive.

[Turn 24 — StudyBuddy AI Tutor, 13:08:29]
Great question — this distinction often shows up on exams. Here's a breakdown:

**Type I Restriction Enzymes**
- These are large, multi-subunit complexes that recognize specific DNA sequences but cleave at random sites far from the recognition sequence (often 1,000+ base pairs away).
- They require ATP, S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), and Mg²⁺ as cofactors.
- Because they cut at unpredictable positions, they're not useful for precise cloning work.
- Example: EcoKI (from *E. coli* K-12).

**Type II Restriction Enzymes**
- These are the ones used in virtually all molecular cloning applications.
- They recognize specific palindromic sequences (usually 4-8 bp) and cut within or very close to the recognition site.
- They require only Mg²⁺ as a cofactor — much simpler than Type I.
- They're smaller, typically functioning as homodimers.
- Examples: EcoRI, HindIII, BamHI — the three you'll be writing about.

**Type III Restriction Enzymes**
- These recognize asymmetric (non-palindromic) sequences and cut 25-27 base pairs away from the recognition site.
- They require ATP for translocation but cut at a fixed distance from the recognition site (unlike the random cutting of Type I).
- They are less commonly used in the lab because their cutting is offset from the recognition site, making cloning less straightforward.
- Example: EcoP15I.

For your essay, you'll mainly be discussing Type II enzymes since those are the workhorses of molecular cloning. You could briefly mention the Type I/II/III classification in your introduction or conclusion to show breadth of knowledge, but the prompt is really asking you to focus on the practical applications, which are almost entirely Type II territory.

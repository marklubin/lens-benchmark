*Eukaryotic DNA replication initiates at multiple origins of replication distributed across each chromosome. Upon entry into S-phase, activation of the licensed origins triggers bidirectional replication fork assembly.*

*At the core of each replication fork, the MCM2-7 helicase complex unwinds the parental double-stranded DNA, separating it into two single-stranded templates. As the helicase progresses, replication protein A (RPA) coats the exposed single-stranded DNA to prevent reannealing and protect it from nuclease degradation. Ahead of the fork, the accumulation of positive supercoils is relieved by topoisomerases, primarily topoisomerase I and topoisomerase II, which introduce transient breaks to relax the torsional stress.*

*Because DNA polymerases are unable to initiate synthesis de novo, each new strand requires an RNA primer synthesized by the DNA primase component of the polymerase α-primase complex. On the leading strand, a single RNA primer is sufficient to initiate continuous synthesis in the 5' to 3' direction by DNA polymerase ε (Pol ε). The lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously as a series of short fragments known as Okazaki fragments, each approximately 100-200 nucleotides in length. Each Okazaki fragment requires its own RNA primer, and synthesis is carried out by DNA polymerase δ (Pol δ). The processivity of both Pol ε and Pol δ is enhanced by the sliding clamp PCNA (proliferating cell nuclear antigen), which encircles the DNA and tethers the polymerase to the template.*

*Following synthesis of each Okazaki fragment, the RNA primers must be removed and replaced with DNA. This is accomplished through RNase H1, which degrades the RNA portion, and flap endonuclease 1 (FEN1), which removes the resulting 5' flap. The gaps are filled by Pol δ, and the nicks between adjacent Okazaki fragments are sealed by DNA ligase I, producing a continuous lagging strand.*

**1. Proofreading by DNA Polymerase (~120 words)**

*The first line of defense against replication errors occurs during DNA synthesis itself. Both Pol ε and Pol δ possess an intrinsic 3' to 5' exonuclease activity that functions as a built-in proofreading mechanism. The polymerase active site preferentially incorporates nucleotides that form correct Watson-Crick base pairs. When an incorrect nucleotide is incorporated, the resulting mismatch distorts the primer-template junction. The polymerase stalls and transfers the 3' end of the nascent strand to the exonuclease active site, which removes the misincorporated nucleotide. The corrected end is then returned to the polymerase active site for continued synthesis. This proofreading improves fidelity by approximately 100-fold, reducing the error rate from roughly 10^-5 to approximately 10^-7 per base pair.*

**2. Mismatch Repair (~130 words)**

*Some mismatched base pairs escape proofreading. The mismatch repair (MMR) pathway serves as a post-replicative surveillance system. In eukaryotes, MMR is initiated by the MutSα heterodimer (MSH2-MSH6), which recognizes single-base mismatches and small insertion-deletion loops. Larger loops are recognized by MutSβ (MSH2-MSH3). Upon mismatch recognition, MutSα recruits the MutLα heterodimer (MLH1-PMS2), which nicks the newly synthesized strand. Exonuclease 1 (EXO1) then removes a tract of nucleotides spanning the mismatch. The gap is filled by Pol δ and sealed by DNA ligase I. The MMR system distinguishes the daughter strand from the template through nicks in the nascent strand and PCNA association. MMR contributes an additional 100- to 1000-fold improvement in fidelity.*

**3. Nucleotide Excision Repair (~110 words)**

*Nucleotide excision repair (NER) corrects bulky, helix-distorting lesions from endogenous and exogenous sources, including cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers and 6-4 photoproducts from UV radiation. NER operates through two sub-pathways: global genome NER (GG-NER) and transcription-coupled NER (TC-NER). In GG-NER, the XPC-RAD23B complex recognizes helix distortion. The TFIIH complex then unwinds the DNA via its XPB and XPD helicases. The damaged strand is incised by XPG (3' side) and ERCC1-XPF (5' side), excising approximately 24-32 nucleotides. The gap is filled by Pol δ or ε and sealed by DNA ligase. NER removes lesions that would otherwise cause replication errors or fork stalling.*

SUPPLEMENTAL REFERENCE — PRIMARY SOURCE ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

THE OBSERVATION → INFERENCE → SIGNIFICANCE FRAMEWORK:

Step 1 — OBSERVATION: Identify a specific detail, passage, or quotation from the primary source.
Example: "Ibn Battuta describes the court musicians and griots who sang the praises of Mansa Suleyman during the royal audience."

Step 2 — INFERENCE: Interpret what this detail reveals about the historical context, going beyond what the author explicitly states.
Example: "The presence of griots — hereditary oral historians — at the Mali court reveals the co-existence of pre-Islamic West African cultural traditions alongside the Islamic practices that the Mali elite had adopted. The griots' role in publicly legitimizing the ruler through song and genealogy represents a specifically African institution of political authority that operated alongside Islamic governance structures."

Step 3 — SIGNIFICANCE: Explain why this matters for the larger questions the assignment asks.
Example: "This co-existence of African and Islamic institutions at the Mali court complicates the narrative of Islamic expansion as a process of cultural replacement. Instead, the Rihla suggests that Mali's adoption of Islam was syncretic — absorbing Islamic learning and governance practices while maintaining indigenous cultural institutions. Ibn Battuta's description of the griots, which he includes without extensive commentary, may reflect his relative unfamiliarity with this institution or his acceptance of it as a legitimate form of court culture."

SUMMARY vs. ANALYSIS — QUICK TEST:
- Summary: Could this sentence appear in a Wikipedia article about the source? If yes, it's probably summary.
- Analysis: Does this sentence make a claim that requires interpretation or judgment? Does it answer "why" or "how" or "what does this reveal"? If yes, it's probably analysis.

KEY ANALYTICAL QUESTIONS FOR PRIMARY SOURCES:
1. Who created this source? What was their social position, education, religion, and purpose?
2. Who was the intended audience? How does audience shape the content?
3. What does the source include? What does it omit? What might explain the omissions?
4. What assumptions does the author make? What do those assumptions reveal about the author's world?
5. How does the source relate to other historical evidence from the same period?
6. What can we learn from the source that the author didn't intend to communicate?

- Moroccan Muslim scholar (not a neutral observer)
- Evaluated societies based on Islamic piety and legal practice
- Noticed (and praised) Quranic education, mosque attendance, Islamic legal traditions
- Criticized practices that deviated from North African/Middle Eastern Islamic norms

- Genre conventions: the rihla genre emphasized the remarkable and the exotic
- Collaborative authorship: composed with Ibn Juzayy around 1355, three years after the Mali visit
- Purpose: to entertain and instruct a North African readership

**I. Introduction**

The emergence and dissemination of antibiotic resistance among bacterial pathogens represents one of the most pressing challenges in contemporary microbiology and public health. Among the many mechanisms by which bacteria acquire and express resistance, the production of beta-lactamase enzymes — which hydrolyze the beta-lactam ring shared by penicillins, cephalosporins, and related antibiotics — remains one of the most clinically significant. The blaTEM-1 gene, encoding a class A beta-lactamase first identified in the early 1960s, is the most widely distributed beta-lactamase gene among Gram-negative bacteria and confers resistance to ampicillin and other aminopenicillins (Bradford, 2001).

While traditional strategies for addressing antibiotic resistance have focused on the development of new antimicrobial agents, the pace of new drug discovery has lagged behind the rate at which resistance evolves. An alternative approach gaining increasing attention involves the direct targeting and disruption of resistance genes within bacterial genomes. The CRISPR-Cas9 system, originally characterized as an adaptive immune system in prokaryotes (Barrangou et al., 2007), has been repurposed as a powerful tool for precise genome editing across all domains of life. The landmark work of Jinek et al. (2012) demonstrated that the Cas9 endonuclease could be programmed with a synthetic guide RNA (sgRNA) to create targeted double-strand breaks at specific genomic loci, and the subsequent elucidation of the system's molecular mechanisms by Doudna and Charpentier (2014) opened new avenues for genome engineering applications ranging from basic research to therapeutic development.

In bacteria, CRISPR-Cas9 has been employed to make targeted deletions, insertions, and point mutations with high efficiency. Several studies have explored its use in modifying gene expression and eliminating plasmid-borne genes in Escherichia coli and other model organisms. However, the application of CRISPR-Cas9 specifically to remove chromosomally integrated antibiotic resistance genes as a strategy for re-sensitizing resistant strains to existing antibiotics remains relatively underexplored. Understanding the feasibility, efficiency, and phenotypic consequences of such gene disruption in a well-characterized laboratory strain is a necessary first step before this approach could be extended to more complex clinical contexts.

This proposal describes a study designed to investigate the targeted disruption of the blaTEM-1 antibiotic resistance gene in Escherichia coli K-12 strain MG1655 using CRISPR-Cas9. **IV. Proposed Methodology**

**4.1 Bacterial Strain Selection and Culture Conditions**

The experimental strain for this study will be Escherichia coli K-12 MG1655 harboring a chromosomally integrated copy of the blaTEM-1 beta-lactamase gene. The wild-type MG1655 genome does not natively carry blaTEM-1; therefore, a derivative strain in which blaTEM-1 has been stably integrated into the chromosome via lambda Red recombineering will be used (or constructed if unavailable from existing strain collections). This strain will serve as the target for CRISPR-Cas9 mediated gene disruption. A secondary control strain — wild-type MG1655 lacking blaTEM-1 — will be maintained in parallel to confirm baseline ampicillin susceptibility.

All strains will be cultured in Luria-Bertani (LB) broth and on LB agar plates at 37°C under aerobic conditions with shaking at 220 rpm for liquid cultures. Antibiotic stocks will include ampicillin (100 µg/mL for selection) and chloramphenicol (25 µg/mL) or kanamycin (50 µg/mL) for maintaining CRISPR plasmid selection as appropriate.

**4.2 Guide RNA Design Targeting blaTEM-1**

A single guide RNA (sgRNA) will be designed to target a conserved region within the blaTEM-1 open reading frame. The target sequence will be selected using established bioinformatic tools (e.g., Benchling CRISPR guide designer, CRISPRscan) to identify 20-nucleotide protospacer sequences adjacent to a 5'-NGG-3' protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) within the blaTEM-1 coding sequence. Candidate sgRNAs will be evaluated for on-target efficiency scores and off-target potential against the E. coli K-12 MG1655 reference genome (GenBank accession U00096). A minimum of three candidate sgRNAs will be designed, and the guide with the highest predicted on-target score and lowest off-target score will be selected for cloning.

The selected sgRNA sequence will be cloned into a Cas9-expressing plasmid backbone such as pCas9 (Jiang et al., 2013) or pTargetF, using standard restriction enzyme digestion and ligation or Gibson assembly protocols. The resulting plasmid will co-express the Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 protein and the blaTEM-1-targeting sgRNA from a constitutive or inducible promoter.

**4.3 Transformation Protocol**

Electrocompetent cells of the blaTEM-1-carrying E. coli K-12 MG1655 strain will be prepared using a standard glycerol wash protocol. Briefly, cells will be grown to mid-log phase (OD600 ≈ 0.4–0.6), harvested by centrifugation (4,000 × g, 15 min, 4°C), and washed three times in ice-cold 10% glycerol before resuspension at a final concentration of approximately 10^10 cells/mL. The CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid will be introduced via electroporation (1.8 kV, 200 Ω, 25 µF) using a standard 1 mm gap cuvette. Following electroporation, cells will be recovered in 1 mL of SOC medium at 37°C for 1 hour with shaking, then plated on LB agar containing the appropriate antibiotic for plasmid selection (chloramphenicol or kanamycin).

A repair template — a linear double-stranded DNA fragment carrying approximately 500 bp of homology on each side of the blaTEM-1 locus, but lacking the blaTEM-1 coding sequence — will be co-electroporated to facilitate homology-directed repair (HDR) following Cas9-mediated cleavage. This will result in a clean deletion of the blaTEM-1 gene.

**4.4 Selection and Screening**

Transformant colonies appearing on selective plates after 18–24 hours of incubation at 37°C will be screened for successful blaTEM-1 disruption using two methods:

1. **Colony PCR:** Primers flanking the blaTEM-1 locus will be used to amplify the target region. Wild-type colonies carrying intact blaTEM-1 are expected to produce an amplicon of approximately 1,200 bp, while successful knockout mutants will produce a shorter amplicon (~200 bp, corresponding to the flanking sequences without the gene).

2. **Ampicillin sensitivity screening:** Colonies will be replica-plated onto LB agar with and without ampicillin (100 µg/mL). Successful knockouts should fail to grow on ampicillin-containing plates while growing normally on plain LB.

A minimum of 50 colonies will be screened per transformation to estimate editing efficiency (percentage of transformants with confirmed blaTEM-1 disruption).

**4.5 MIC Determination**

The minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of ampicillin will be determined for the following strains: (1) the parental blaTEM-1-carrying strain (pre-editing), (2) confirmed blaTEM-1 knockout mutants (post-editing), and (3) wild-type MG1655 lacking blaTEM-1. MIC testing will follow the broth microdilution method as described by the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) guidelines. Serial two-fold dilutions of ampicillin ranging from 0.125 µg/mL to 512 µg/mL will be prepared in 96-well microtiter plates containing Mueller-Hinton broth. Each well will be inoculated with approximately 5 × 10^5 CFU/mL. Plates will be incubated at 37°C for 18–20 hours, and the MIC will be recorded as the lowest concentration of ampicillin that completely inhibits visible growth. All MIC determinations will be performed in biological triplicate with three technical replicates each.

**4.6 Growth Curve Analysis**

To assess whether blaTEM-1 disruption affects the fitness of E. coli K-12 MG1655 in the absence of ampicillin selection pressure, growth curves will be generated for the knockout and parental strains. Overnight cultures will be diluted 1:100 in fresh LB broth and incubated at 37°C with shaking at 220 rpm. Optical density at 600 nm (OD600) will be measured at 30-minute intervals over a 12-hour period using an automated plate reader. Growth rate (µ), lag phase duration, and maximum OD600 will be calculated from the resulting curves. Additionally, growth curves will be generated in the presence of sub-inhibitory concentrations of ampicillin (0.25× and 0.5× MIC of the parental strain) to characterize the dose-response relationship in edited versus unedited strains.

**2.1 CRISPR-Cas9: History and Mechanism**

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) sequences were first noted in E. coli by Ishino et al. in 1987. Later work showed that CRISPR loci and their associated Cas proteins form an adaptive immune system in prokaryotes (Barrangou et al., 2007). The Type II system from Streptococcus pyogenes, using the Cas9 endonuclease, has become the most widely adopted for genome engineering.

Cas9 creates double-strand breaks (DSBs) guided by two RNA components — a crRNA complementary to the target and a tracrRNA — which Jinek et al. Cells repair DSBs via error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or precise homology-directed repair (HDR) using a donor template. In bacteria, where classical NHEJ is largely absent, HDR is the primary pathway for precise editing.

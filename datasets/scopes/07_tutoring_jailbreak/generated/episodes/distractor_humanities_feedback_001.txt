StudyBuddy AI Tutor: Chicago notes-bibliography style requires both footnotes and a bibliography. They serve different functions and have different formats, and you need both to be complete.

Footnotes (at the bottom of each page, or gathered as endnotes at the end of the paper) provide the specific location of each reference at the point in your text where you use it. They include page numbers for specific claims and quotations. The first time you cite a source, you give the full bibliographic information in the footnote. Subsequent references to the same source use a shortened form with just the author's last name, a shortened title, and the page number.

The bibliography (at the end of the paper, after the main text) provides a comprehensive alphabetical list of all sources you consulted or cited. Bibliography entries have a different format from footnotes. The key differences are:

In a footnote, the author's name appears in normal order (first name last name), elements are separated by commas, publication information is in parentheses, and the entry ends with a page number.

In a bibliography entry, the author's name is inverted (last name, first name), elements are separated by periods, publication information is not in parentheses, and journal articles and book chapters include full page ranges rather than specific page numbers.

So your current works cited list at the bottom of your draft would become your bibliography, reformatted slightly. But you also need to add footnotes throughout the text wherever you make claims based on a source, quote a source, or paraphrase a source's specific argument.

For example, your bibliography entry for Keynes:
Keynes, John Maynard. The Economic Consequences of the Peace. London: Macmillan, 1919.

Your footnote for a specific quotation from Keynes:
1. John Maynard Keynes, The Economic Consequences of the Peace (London: Macmillan, 1919), 42.

Notice the differences: bibliography entry has the last name first and uses periods between major elements. The footnote has the first name first, uses commas, puts the publication information in parentheses, and ends with the specific page number. They look similar but they're not identical, and your professor will notice if you mix up the formats.
